## [SECTION 1: What Is AI? 1:30–4:00]

**Visual:** Diagram of AI types (narrow vs. general AI) with examples.

**Script:**
> Let's start with the basics: what is AI?
>
> AI, or Artificial Intelligence, refers to machines performing tasks that typically require human intelligence — like:
> - Understanding language (e.g., reading a donor report).
> - Recognizing patterns (e.g., analyzing beneficiary feedback).
> - Making decisions (e.g., prioritizing grant opportunities).
>
> For CSOs, we're focusing on narrow AI: tools designed for specific tasks, like:
> - **Natural Language Processing (NLP):** understanding and generating text — chatbots, summarization.
> - **Predictive analytics:** forecasting trends — donor behavior, project outcomes.
> - **Automation:** handling repetitive tasks — data entry, report drafting.
>
> Example: imagine an AI tool that automatically extracts key metrics from your M&E data and drafts a progress report. That's narrow AI in action.
>
> What AI is not:
> - It's not a replacement for human judgment — it won't decide your NGO's strategy.
> - It's not magic — it's a tool that learns from data, and sometimes makes mistakes.
> - It's not just for big organizations — many AI tools are free or low-cost.

## [SECTION 2: How CSOs Are Using AI Today 4:00–8:00]

**Visual:** Screenshots of real CSO workflows (log frames, donor reports, M&E data).

**Script:**
> Now let's look at how CSOs are already using AI — and how you can too.
>
> **1. Grant writing**
> - Problem: writing proposals is time-consuming and requires donor-specific language.
> - AI solution: tools like Claude can draft sections of proposals based on past successful applications, and align with donor priorities (EU, USAID) by analyzing their guidelines.
> - Example: a manual grant draft for a youth employment project takes about ten hours with multiple revisions. An AI-assisted draft takes about two hours, with a higher-quality first pass.
>
> **2. Monitoring & Evaluation (M&E)**
> - Problem: analyzing qualitative data — beneficiary feedback — is labor-intensive.
> - AI solution: summarize feedback from hundreds of surveys in minutes, identify trends ("80% of beneficiaries report improved skills"), and flag gaps ("lack of feedback from rural women").
>
> **3. Donor reporting**
> - Problem: reporting is repetitive and donor-specific.
> - AI solution: auto-generate compliance narratives from project data and format reports to match donor templates.
>
> Key takeaway: AI isn't replacing your expertise — it's augmenting it, freeing up time for high-impact work.

## [SECTION 3: AI Myths for CSOs 8:00–10:00]

**Visual:** Text on screen: "Myth vs. Reality" with icons.

**Script:**
> Before we go deeper, let's debunk three myths about AI in CSOs.
>
> **Myth 1: AI is too expensive for NGOs.**
> Reality: many AI tools are free or low-cost — Claude's free tier handles most text tasks, Google Sheets with AI add-ons covers data analysis, Canva's AI tools handle design.
>
> **Myth 2: AI requires technical expertise.**
> Reality: you don't need to code. Prompt engineering — which we'll cover in Lesson 2 — is the most important skill. Instead of "write a report," you'll learn to say: "You are a project manager for a human rights NGO. Summarize our Q2 2024 achievements in three bullet points, focusing on impact for 100+ beneficiaries."
>
> **Myth 3: AI will replace CSO jobs.**
> Reality: AI automates tasks, not jobs. Your role shifts toward strategic oversight, designing better workflows, and focusing on impact.
>
> Bottom line: AI is a tool to enhance your work, not replace it.
